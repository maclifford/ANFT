For twenty years we said the forest is the therapist. That sentence served us; it also said slightly too much and slightly too little. Too much, because the forest doesn’t practice therapy — clinicians do, and now they train with us. Too little, because it cast the forest as a provider of services, when what we’ve learned is that it’s a partner in relationship. What we understand now is this:

The forest is the landscape of discovery — a landscape of threshold invitations.

Amos Clifford, Founder
What This Is

A Living System

ANFT is not a school with students. It is something that has grown — more or less organically, over more than a decade — out of the interaction of many hands, many places, and one shared practice. This page describes what has grown. None of it is aspiration; all of it is already here.

What has grown

Since the first cohort walked together in 2014, certified guides have taken this practice into more than 65 countries. The cohorts themselves carry names — Komorebi, Cedar, Sea Otter — and numbers now deep into the hundreds, a lineage each guide belongs to permanently. Guides do not simply receive a curriculum; they create new invitations, adapt the practice to their own cultures and landscapes, and return what they learn to a shared body of knowledge that every later cohort inherits.

Around the guiding practice, other forms of contribution took root. Trained consultants now work with land stewards to certify forest therapy trails, spaces, and places — a growing registry of lands, across continents, in formal relationship with this work. Health care professionals carry the practice into clinics through Nature as Medicine. Practitioners and organizations bring offerings of their own through partnership. Walks are guided, and reported, and those reports accumulate into a record of what happens when people and places meet with attention.

No central office produced this. The organization’s role has been to hold the standard, tend the commons, and open the doors — and through those doors, people and places have built the rest.

How the system feeds itself

Each contribution enriches what every other member draws from. A guide’s new invitation, shared with the community, becomes part of the practice a cohort in another hemisphere learns next year. A newly certified trail gives every nearby guide a place to work, and gives its stewards a global community of practitioners who will care for it. A clinician’s outcomes strengthen the case that opens institutional doors for everyone. The directory that helps the public find a guide is made valuable by every guide in it — and makes every guide in it easier to find. This is what it means to say the benefits co-emerge: they are not delivered from the center outward, but arise between the members, the practice, and the land.

The land is a member

From the beginning, this community’s motto has made an unusual claim: the Forest is the Therapist; the Guide opens the Doors. Taken seriously — and we take it seriously — that means the land is not the venue for this work but a participant in it. The healing that happens on a walk is the land’s contribution. The character of each certified trail is authored by the place itself and merely recognized by the certification. The venues that hold our immersions are in long-term relationship with us, not under contract to us. What the land gives, the system owes back: care, reciprocity, and standing. That obligation shapes how this organization intends to grow.

Open doors

A living system has many entrances, and no single correct one. Some arrive wanting to guide; some arrive as physicians or therapists; some arrive holding responsibility for a piece of land; some arrive with a work of their own that wants making; some arrive with an offering to bring. The five academies are five doors into the same commons — and the Forest Threshold is the ground they all open onto. Whichever door you enter, you are not enrolling in a program. You are taking a place in something alive, which will be different because you are in it.

About Us

We Are ANFT

Advancing relational forest therapy through education, practice, research, and global community.

Founded in 2012 by Amos Clifford, the Association of Nature and Forest Therapy emerged from a deep recognition that humanity’s disconnection from nature has profound consequences for our health, wellbeing, and the planet itself. Drawing inspiration from the Japanese practice of shinrin-yoku — forest bathing — Amos developed a unique methodology that combines ancient wisdom with contemporary science. As our mission has expanded beyond guide training, ANFT is evolving into the Academies of Nature and Forest Therapies: distinct learning paths that reflect the many ways people reconnect with the living world.

Today we are a thriving global community spanning more than 70 countries, with over 3,000 certified Forest Therapy Guides. Our network includes health care professionals, educators, conservationists, and wellness practitioners, united by a shared commitment to reconnecting people with the healing power of nature. Through this work, millions of hours of nature connection have created ripples of healing that extend from individuals to communities to ecosystems.

Our values are rooted in inclusion, evidence-based practice, and deep respect for both nature and human diversity. We believe access to nature connection is fundamental to individual and planetary wellbeing, and we work to make this practice accessible across cultures, professions, and communities.

Why ANFT?

Our approach combines scientifically supported health research with a deeply relational philosophy. Through a structured yet adaptable framework, guides learn to help others slow down, awaken their senses, and enter into meaningful relationship with nature, self, and place. At ANFT, the guide is not the therapist — the forest is the therapist. More than a professional certification, this is a pathway into deeper relationship with the living world.
